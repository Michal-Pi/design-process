# Buxton: Sketching User Experiences

Principles from Bill Buxton's foundational work on the role of sketches in design ideation.

## Core Principles

**Sketching is ideation, not documentation.** A sketch is a cognitive tool for exploring
possibilities — not a deliverable. The moment a wireframe becomes a deliverable, it stops
serving its primary purpose: helping the designer think.

**Physical attributes of sketches (the 5 properties):**
1. **Quick** — fast enough to match the pace of thought
2. **Timely** — produced at the right stage (before commitment)
3. **Inexpensive** — low cost per attempt = high exploration rate
4. **Disposable** — no psychological investment in any single sketch
5. **Abundant** — quantity enables comparison and improvement

**Low cost enables high exploration.** The critical insight: if each sketch costs 5 hours,
a designer will produce 3 sketches per week. If each sketch costs 5 minutes, the same
designer produces 60+ per week. The design space explored grows exponentially with speed.

**Sketches invite critique; renderings invite admiration.** A polished wireframe signals
"done" and discourages iteration. A rough sketch signals "in progress" and invites
collaborative improvement. Lo-fi fidelity is a deliberate communication signal.

**Specificity constraint:** A sketch should be no more specific than the decision being made.
If the question is "which layout serves this task?", the sketch should only answer that
question — not color, not typography, not component choice.

## Application to complete-design Stage 3

Stage 3 wireframes implement these principles:
- Excalidraw's hand-drawn rendering style signals "sketch" not "spec"
- FID-03 (no color/fonts) enforces the specificity constraint: only structure is decided
- Crazy 8s forces 8 variants (quantity → comparison → better decisions)
- CHOICE.md captures the rationale before fidelity increases in Stage 4
