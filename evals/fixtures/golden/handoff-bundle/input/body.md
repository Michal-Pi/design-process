## Goal & scope

Stage 1 research synthesis for a SaaS project management tool targeting mid-market teams of 10 to 50 people. The research methodology combined semi-structured interviews (5 participants, 45 minutes each), a 5-day diary study with a 40 percent completion rate, and a competitive landscape analysis covering 8 existing tools. The competitive analysis covered Linear, Notion, Asana, Monday, Jira, ClickUp, Basecamp, and Todoist. The analysis identified three dominant IA patterns in the competitive landscape: project-centric (Linear, Jira, Asana), workspace-centric (Notion, ClickUp), and team-centric (Basecamp). None of the 8 competitors surfaces context externalization as a top-level navigation concept. This is the design space we are targeting. The research phase produced 3 validated personas and 2 proto-grade personas. The proto-grade personas are based on interview synthesis and competitive analysis inference; they have not been validated with individual participants. The research team made a deliberate decision to ship proto-grade personas rather than delay Stage 1 to achieve full validation, on the grounds that Stage 2 IA decisions can proceed with the validated primary persona as the anchor and the proto-grade personas as hypotheses to be tested. This decision is documented in the Stage 1 gate record and will be reviewed at the Stage 2 gate. The research phase spanned four weeks and produced 3 validated personas and 2 proto-grade personas derived from semi-structured user interviews, diary studies, and competitive analysis. The primary research question was: what is the actual cognitive burden of current tool fragmentation, and how does it manifest differently for PMs, ICs, and team leads? The research surfaced a clear hierarchy: tool fragmentation is the root cause, notification overload is a symptom, and context loss across tools is the user's daily experience of the problem. The core JTBD distilled from interview analysis: "When I am switching between my project tracker, my chat tool, and my documentation system, I want to know immediately where I left off and what requires my attention, so I can stop reconstructing context from scratch every time." This JTBD shapes the entire Stage 2 IA strategy: the top-level sitemap must externalize context, not just present task lists. Interview quota was n=5; recommended for enterprise SaaS is n=8. The gap is documented as a known risk and the Stage 2 team is expected to run 2 additional validation interviews before the Stage 2 gate. All five interviews were conducted by the same researcher to minimize inter-rater bias. Sessions averaged 47 minutes. All participants gave written consent for research use. No PII is stored in the design directory.

## Methodology note

The research synthesis document consolidates findings from three data sources: interview transcripts, diary study entries, and competitive analysis. The weights applied to each source in the synthesis are: interview transcripts 60 percent, diary study entries 25 percent, competitive analysis 15 percent. These weights reflect the research team's confidence in each source type: interviews are the most direct evidence of user mental models; diary entries provide longitudinal context but are subject to recall bias and low completion; competitive analysis provides structural context but does not directly evidence user needs. All interview transcripts were coded by a single researcher using an affinity diagram methodology. The affinity diagram produced 12 first-order clusters and 4 second-order themes. The 4 themes are: context loss, notification overload, tool fragmentation, and reporting overhead. The primary persona (Alex) maps primarily to context loss and tool fragmentation. The secondary personas map to notification overload and reporting overhead respectively. The JTBD hierarchy was derived from the 12 first-order clusters using a jobs-to-be-done ladder method based on the Klement framework. The full JTBD ladder is in design/research/jtbd-synthesis.md.

## Decisions made

First decision: adopted Indi Young thinking-style persona format over Cooper-Goodwin demographic-goal format. Rationale: thinking-style personas are more durable across product pivots and map more cleanly to the JTBD-first research methodology we used. The Cooper-Goodwin format would have required us to invent demographic details that our screener intentionally avoided. The thinking-style format captures how users mentally model and navigate work, which is directly relevant to IA decisions in Stage 2. This decision was unanimous among the research team. Second decision: rejected tool-specific mental models in persona descriptions. All five interviewees described their mental models in activity-level terms ("planning work vs. doing work"), not tool-level terms ("Slack vs. Linear"). Encoding tool-specific mental models would couple the design to the current tool landscape and reduce the persona shelf-life. Third decision: chose LATCH IA foundation (Location plus Alphabetical plus Time plus Category plus Hierarchy) for the Stage 2 sitemap. Of the five LATCH primitives, Time (sprint cycles) and Category (by project vs. by person vs. by status) are the two strongest signals from interviews. Location and Alphabetical are likely to be used at lower hierarchy levels. This decision is provisional; it may be revised based on card-sort results in Stage 2. Fourth decision: validated that mobile usage is secondary. Four of five interviewees said they use the tool on desktop 90% or more of the time. The one mobile-heavy user is a field PM whose context differs significantly from the primary persona. Mobile-first IA is deferred to v2.1. Fifth decision: agreed that notification taxonomy requires a dedicated Stage 2 investigation. No strong consensus emerged from Stage 1 interviews. Two participants prefer per-project notification scoping; two prefer a unified cross-project inbox; one participant uses email forwarding as their primary notification surface. The Stage 2 task is to prototype both patterns and run a preference test.

## Open questions

Question one: should the IA distinguish between solo users and team leads at the top level? Evidence from interviews is genuinely mixed. Three of five interviewees said they switch between solo contributor and team lead roles within the same week, sometimes within the same day. A top-level role split would force users to choose a mode upfront, which conflicts with their actual usage pattern. A role-adaptive IA (same structure, contextually surfaced features) may be more appropriate but is architecturally harder. This question must be resolved before the Stage 2 gate. Recommendation: prototype a single-IA approach with progressive disclosure of team-lead features based on team membership, and test with 2 team-lead interviewees in Stage 2.

Question two: notification taxonomy — per-project versus cross-project inbox pattern. As documented in the decisions section, no consensus. The Stage 2 recommendation is to prototype both and run a 5-person unmoderated preference test. The winner should be validated before Stage 3.

Question three: should the Stage 2 sitemap differentiate between onboarding and returning-user paths? One interviewee in the enterprise segment described a multi-week onboarding curve with a steep learning cliff. This suggests the IA should have an explicit "getting started" pathway that diverges from the returning-user flow. The other four interviewees did not raise onboarding as a concern, possibly because they adopted the tool organically. This is a medium-priority open question for Stage 2.

Question four: how should the tool handle asynchronous team members in distributed teams? Two interviewees work with teams across more than two time zones. The "context loss" problem is significantly worse for them because they cannot rely on real-time synchronization. This may require a dedicated "async handoff" feature surface that is not captured in the current primary persona.

## Artifacts inventory

First artifact: personas/p1.json — primary persona, validated, provenance grade validated. Alex is a mid-market PM at a 25-person software team. Thinking style: separates planning work from doing work mentally; gets frustrated when tools blur this boundary. Time orientation: thinks in 2-week sprint cycles; past-focused for blockers, future-focused for capacity. Social processing: processes priorities alone before team sync; does not like being put on the spot. Three primary JTBDs: stop losing context when switching between Slack, Linear, and Notion mid-sprint; know at a glance which team member is blocked, without asking in Slack; export a clean sprint report for stakeholders in under 5 minutes.

Second artifact: ASSUMPTIONS.md — research assumptions documented. Validated assumptions: target user is a mid-market PM (10 to 50 person team), confirmed via screener in all 5 interviews; primary pain is tool fragmentation not task management per se, confirmed via JTBD analysis; mobile usage is secondary, confirmed by 4 of 5 interviewees. Proto-grade assumptions: enterprise buyers need SSO, assumed from market research not direct interviews; notification fatigue is the top frustration, inferred from interview tone not directly measured. Out-of-scope assumptions: we are not assuming a specific tech stack for the product; we are not validating pricing model assumptions in Stage 1.

Rungs 2, 4, 6, and 7 may be relevant for secondary persona development in Stage 2 and for feature prioritization in Stage 3.

## Provenance (worst-case)

The provenance worst-case for this bundle is proto. Two personas are PROTO-grade because they were generated from interview synthesis without individual validation sessions. The primary persona (p1.json, Alex) is validated grade because it was validated against all 5 interview transcripts and received explicit confirmation from 3 of 5 participants in a follow-up persona review session. The secondary personas have not gone through the validation step. The Stage 2 handoff must not promote these to VALIDATED without additional interview evidence. Stage 2 IA decisions derived from proto-grade personas must carry forward the proto provenance grade in their own artifact frontmatter.

## Risks surfaced

Risk one: proto-grade personas may introduce synthetic bias into the IA hierarchy if used as the primary signal for Stage 2 card sort design. Risk level is medium. Mitigation: schedule 2 additional interviews before the Stage 2 gate; use only the validated primary persona as the basis for the Stage 2 sitemap structure until additional personas are validated. The proto-grade personas should be treated as hypotheses, not facts. Each proto-grade assumption listed in ASSUMPTIONS.md should have a corresponding Stage 2 test or interview probe. The research team must document which Stage 2 decisions are downstream of proto-grade personas and which are downstream of validated personas. This provenance chain must be preserved in Stage 2 artifact frontmatter.

Risk two: interview sample (n=5) is below the recommended n=8 for enterprise SaaS research per the Cooper-Goodwin rubric. Risk: underrepresentation of enterprise-tier users who have different notification needs and a longer onboarding curve. Mitigation: add an enterprise-tier screener criterion for the next wave of research; ensure at least 2 of the 3 additional interviews are with enterprise-segment users. Track recruitment progress in the Stage 2 plan. If recruitment fails, document the deviation in the Stage 2 gate evidence and request a waiver from the design lead.

Risk three: the decision to use thinking-style personas over demographic personas may reduce the cross-functional usability of the research artifacts for the engineering team and the marketing team, who are accustomed to demographic persona formats. Mitigation: provide a one-page persona translation document that maps thinking-style dimensions to the demographic fields that engineering and marketing typically use. The translation document should be reviewed by at least one engineering lead and one marketing lead before Stage 2 gate. If the translation document is not created, the risk escalates to high because engineering will be building IA from personas they do not understand how to use.

Risk four: the competitive analysis was conducted on a 90-day-old snapshot of the competitive landscape. Three new entrants have shipped features since the snapshot was taken. Risk: the IA decisions may not adequately differentiate from new competitive moves. Mitigation: the product manager should review the competitive landscape at Stage 2 kickoff and flag any material changes to the research synthesis before the Stage 2 sitemap is finalized. If a competitive entrant has shipped a noticeably similar IA structure, the design team should consider a differentiation session before Stage 2 gate.

Risk five: the diary study component of the research had a 40 percent dropout rate (3 of 5 participants completed the 5-day diary). The dropout rate introduces a potential selection bias: the participants who completed the diary may be more diligent or more engaged with their tools than the broader target population. Mitigation: the research synthesis should weight diary-derived insights with a lower confidence flag. Insights that rely primarily on diary data should be marked as medium-confidence rather than high-confidence. The Stage 2 team should be aware of this limitation when using the research to justify IA decisions.
